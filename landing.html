02How it works

Three motions.
Then you're listening.

Console-7 doesn't give you a search bar. It gives you a knob, a band, and a length. The interaction is the entertainment.

00-1 · TUNE

Pick a source.

Drag the rotary dial or click a station marker. The knob snaps to the four sources — AI, Web3, Breaking News, Weather. No search bar. No infinite scroll.

00-2 · SET TIME

Tell it how long.

12 minutes. 45 minutes. Two hours. The model writes to fit. Headlines compress for short windows, deeper context unfolds for longer ones.

00-3 · LISTEN

Hit play.

The next show is generated while you're listening to the current one. Continuous, never repeating. Walk out the door — it'll be ready by the time the headphones are on.

Console-7's shows are not pre-recorded. AURA-3 reads your sources, picks what's worth saying, writes a script, and hands it to ElevenLabs to voice. Every dial-in is a one-of-one.

06Operator's FAQ

Read before broadcast.

Short answers to the questions that come up before the first invite. Long answers in the handbook — and live, in the GitHub issues.

06-1 What's actually playing?

Generated audio. AURA-3 reads your sources, picks the items worth covering, writes a script, and ElevenLabs voices it (podcastfy on the open-source path). Nothing is pre-recorded; nothing is stitched from licensed clips.

06-2 Where do the sources come from?

Your X timeline, your podcast subscriptions, news wires, and weather services. We pull, dedupe, and rank against a per-listener taste vector. Skip and dwell signals tune it; nothing is shared back across users.

06-3 How does it fit my commute?

You set a length — 12 minutes, 45 minutes, two hours. The model writes to that target. Short windows compress to headlines and one deep dive; long windows unfold context, voices, and breathing room.

06-4 Why a knob? It's the web.

Because radio is exploration, not search. The dial gives you serendipity, accidents, the feeling of finding a station. A search bar collapses that into a query. We are not building a query tool.

06-5 Why amber? Why beige?

It's how machines used to look when machines were trustworthy. Mission control consoles, hi-fi receivers, lab equipment — the warmth was on purpose. Our software is supposed to feel like that, not like a notification.

06-6 "Built in public" — what does that actually mean?

The repo is public from day one. Every commit, every blocker, every near-pivot. We started Saturday at 10 AM and we'll keep posting through ship. If you want to watch software get made, watch the repo.
